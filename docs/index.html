The Ocean's Fuel: How Sea Surface Temperature Shapes Atlantic Hurricanes

Where does the ocean provide the most fuel for storm intensity?

Focused on the Atlantic hurricane basin (10-45°N, 100-15°W) during the 2020 season.

August 2020
26.5°C

Design Rationale

We wanted to answer one question: where does the ocean provide the most fuel for Atlantic hurricane intensification? We focused on the 2020 Atlantic hurricane season because it was the most active with 31 named storms, which gave us the densest possible set of storm tracks to work with. We intentionally scoped the visualization for only the Atlantic basin, since adding the Pacific or Indian ocean would've introduced noise since those systems have completely different dynamics, and the assignment needed a more focused design.

For the visual encodings, the SST is shown as a grid of small colored rectangles, with each representing a 0.25° lat/lon cell. The color scale runs from dark purple (coolest, ~18°C) through blue, cyan, green, yellow, orange, to red (warmest, ~30°C+). Cells below 26.5°C are dimmed so the hurricane fuel zones can visually pop. We chose this encoding because the warm/cool boundary is the entire point of the visualization and it allows viewers to immediately see which ocean regions are above the threshold. Hurricane tracks are drawn as lines colored by each storm's peak Saffir-Simpson category, with circles at each storm sized by wind speed so that its intensification is visible along the path. Each circle represents a recorded storm position, with the circle color showing the storm's overall peak category, while the circle size shows the wind speed at that specific moment. In this way, the viewers can see the storm strengthening or weakening along its path.

For the interactions, the "Month" slider lets you go through months June to November of 2020 to watch the SST evolve across the hurricane season. The temperature threshold slider ("Highlight water above (°C)") lets users adjust the fuel zone cutoff themselves which immediately updates which cells are more visually striking. The "Play Season" button animates through the months automatically. The "Show storm tracks" checkbox lets viewers toggle between looking at just the SST map on its own versus seeing where the storms actually traveled, which helps isolate each layer of information. Hovering over storm tracks dims the others so you can focus on one storm at a time, and hovering ocean cells shows the exact SST and whether it's above or below threshold. These interactions allow viewers to investigate if each storm was over warm water when it got stronger, which is something a static image can't do.

The storm point tooltip is where the two datasets directly connect. Hovering a storm fix shows the storm name, category, timestamp, wind speed, the SST (and if it is above the fuel threshold) directly underneath that point at that moment. For example in one of the storm fixes, it shows that at one point of time Hurricane Iota was in the "Cat 4" Intensity with 125 kt (knot) winds and was sitting over 28.4°C water, which is well above the 26.5°C threshold. When a storm track goes outside the GOES-16 satellite coverage zone, the tooltip shows "SST below: n/a" which is informative because it shows the limits of the satellite coverage. This approach lets viewers discover the connection between the SST and peak category/intensity by themselves.

Data Transformations

The raw GOES-16 data required several transformations to work in the browser. We clipped to the Atlantic basin (10°N to 45°N, 100°W to 15°W), kept only pixels with a quality flag of 0 (which meant that it was good data), and reprojected from GOES native scan-angle coordinates to a regular 0.25° lat/lon grid. The temperatures were converted from Kelvin to Celsius and wind speeds were converted to the Saffir-Simpson categories.

For the time aggregations, we approximated the monthly mean SST by averaging 24 hourly snapshots across 4 evenly spaced sample days per month, rather than doing all 720 hourly snapshots to keep the data download manageable. We validated this approach since the mean absolute difference was only 0.39°C, and because this is below the visible discrimination threshold of the color scale (0.5°C), the visualization is robust to this approximation.

Our Development Process

Our team of three split the work as follows. Shriya handled the full data pipeline by writing Python scripts to pull GOES-16 SST data from Amazon S3 and HURDAT2 hurricane track data from NOAA, processing everything in Google Colab, and building the base D3 visualization with the SST grid, hurricane tracks, and core controls. She also set up the GitHub repo and GitHub Pages deployment. Anna added the interaction layer with the play button, SST cell hover tooltips, storm track highlighting and dimming, hurricane category legend, and a fade effect on the temperature threshold slider. Shourya constructed the write-up portion of the project.

It's also worth noting that our research question evolved after the checkpoint. Originally we framed the question around whether hurricanes are becoming more intense over time, but after exploring the data more we narrowed it to a more focused and answerable question: where does the ocean provide the most fuel for storm intensity in a single season? This allowed us to build a tighter, more compelling visualization rather than trying to show a lot of things all at once. The 2020 season was the best choice since it was the most active Atlantic season on record.

The total estimated time the project took was approximately 15 people-hours. The most time consuming parts were the data pipeline (GOES files are large and required significant processing to get into a format that was readable for the browser) and building the base D3 map with proper geographic projection.
